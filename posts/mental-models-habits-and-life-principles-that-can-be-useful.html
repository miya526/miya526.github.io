Mental Models, Habits, and Life Principles That Can Be Useful

Life Psychology
November 5, 2024

Last week, during a 1 on 1 with my coworker, we started talking about objective functions in life, systems for decision making, and brought up the 37% rule from the book Algorithms to Live By. Since then, I've been asking myself: what are the rules or principles I try to apply in my life so far? In this post, I’m compiling some of the ones I try to live by. I’ve loosely grouped them by themes for better readability!


Decision Making & Mental Clarity

  • The 37% Rule
    Spend the first 37% of your decision making window exploring options, then choose the next one that’s better than everything you've seen so far. It’s useful framework for situations like job hunts or apartment searches—especially when you're unsure what you want. That said, it’s not meant to be followed blindly. Like many algorithmic rules, it’s most powerful when balanced with intuition and your own sense of timing.
    Source: Algorithms to Live By
  • The Time Horizon Rule
    Ask yourself: How will I feel about this in a few minutes? A few months? A few years? At the end of my life? This simple shift helps you zoom out and put your choices into perspective, especially when emotions are running high. I use it for key decisions in my life, but also for figuring out wether or not to buy certain things.
    Source: Many people probably said this
  • First Principles Thinking
    Break problems into their fundamental truths and reason up from there. Great for innovation and problem solving.
    Source: Aristotle, but Tesla dude made it popular
  • Reframing
    How you interpret a situation influences your emotional response. Reframing helps reduce stress and increase control.
    Source: Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT)
  • Multiple Perspectives Rule
    Most disagreements aren’t about truth vs. lie. They’re about different lived experiences. Be aware of biases.
    Source: Many people probably said this

Habits, Action & Productivity

  • 好记性不如烂笔头 (A good memory is not as reliable as a lousy pen)
    Just write things down instead of trying to remember everything. Take notes, make a to do list, etc.
    Source: Chinese saying
  • Just Do It
    Just do it.
    Source: Nike
  • Parkinson’s Law
    Work expands to fill the time available. Give yourself tighter deadlines to work efficiently.
    Source: C. Northcote Parkinson
  • 滴水穿石 (Dripping water wears through stone)
    Small improvements each day compound into massive change over time.
    Source: Chinese saying
  • Keep Promises to Yourself
    When you follow through on your own word, you build internal trust and self-respect.
    Source: Many people probably said this

Relationships & Communication

  • The Golden Rule
    Treat others the way you’d want to be treated.
    Source: Many people probably said this
  • 以心伝心 (Heart to heart transmission)
    True connection goes beyond language. Listen with the intent to understand, not reply. Creates deeper empathy and connection.
    Japan proverb
  • Speak Their Love Language
    Understand how others feel loved through words, time, gifts, touch, or service.
    Source: Brené Brown

Inner Work & Self-Awareness

  • Internal Family Systems
    We’re made of multiple inner parts. Understand them instead of judging or suppressing. Learning about IFS has been one of the most helpful things for understanding the self better.
    Source: Richard Schwartz
  • 知足常乐 (Contentment brings constant happiness)
    Gratitude and acceptance nurture inner peace. Keeping a gratitude journal is a good way to feel happier
    Source: Chinese proverb
  • Focus on What You Can Control
    Let go of what you can’t control. Focus on what’s within your influence.
    Source: Stoicism maybe?
  • 自業自得 (You reap what you sow)
    You live with the results of your own actions.
    Source: Japanese proverb

Health & Movement

  • Prioritize Sleep
    Make sure to sleep. 7–9 hours of quality sleep is essential for recovery, focus, mood, and health.
    Source: Many people probably said this
  • 腹八分目 (Eat Until 80% Full)
    Habit to stop eating before you're completely full. Promotes longevity and awareness. I think this is also research-backed now.
    Source: Japanese proverb
  • Exercise is a Keystone Habit
    Regular movement creates ripple effects across your life: better mood, discipline, and health.
    Source: Many people probably said this
  • Never Miss Twice
    Missing once is okay. Missing twice builds the wrong habit.
    Source: James Clear

As I was organizing these, a few things came to mind:

  • My worldview is shaped and limited by my own experience. It's bounded by the three languages I speak and the three cultures I'm familiar with. I'm grateful to have been raised across different cultures, and I also wonder what I would be like if I was raised and borned in completely different environment.
  • Experience matters, but it's not enough. What really counts is how openly we experience. If I were a machine learning model, new data would only be useful if it actually updated my parameters. Otherwise, it's just noise.
  • Many ideas are just variations on the same wisdom. James Clear’s 1% rule, for example, is basically the Chinese saying: “Dripping water wears through stone.”

That last point makes me wonder: what the heck is originality? Much of what we call “original” is just remixing and resurfacing of ancient truths in modern form. Many pop psychology books are essentially old proverbs translated into a different language, wrapped in fresh stories and backed by neuroscience or some case studies. It’s not that the insights are new; it’s that they’re made newly legible to us.

And perhaps that’s the difference between math and life advice. In mathematics, theorems are precise and universal. They’re often named after their discoverers because they can’t be restated without preserving their form. Math is a rigorous language—one concept, one structure, no ambiguity. But human language, and life itself, is messier. A single idea can wear a thousand faces, depending on who’s speaking and who’s listening.

Maybe that’s why life wisdom isn’t owned but shared. Passed down, picked up, forgotten, remembered, reworded. And that’s kind of beautiful. The value isn’t just in discovering new truths, but in reconnecting with old ones at the right time, in the right voice. We don’t need brand new rules. We need reminders that land.

So if a rule resonates with you, use it. Make it yours. Call it by whatever name helps it stick. Because whether it comes from a Zen monk, a stoic emperor, a random dude on the internet, or your coworker on a Tuesday, the wisdom is still good. It’s not about where it came from. It’s about whether it helps you live a better life.

With that, I end with a extremely weeby gif from My Hero Academia, showing how the power of One For All is passed down from generation to generation—each successor adding their strength, making it more powerful over time. To me, it’s a perfect symbol of human progress: wisdom accumulated, reshaped, and carried forward, growing stronger with each handoff.
